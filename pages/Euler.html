Turbulent flows are typically present in low viscosity fluids and so can be modelled by the incompressible Euler equations:

\(\hspace{2cm}\begin{align} &\partial_t v + \nabla\cdot (v\otimes v) + \nabla p = 0,\\ &\nabla\cdot (v) = 0 \end{align}\)

The flows are characterised by rapid and chaotic changes in a fluid's velocity and so can be tricky to analyse. One method for addressing turbulence is through Reynold's averaging where we take advantage of Laminar flows that profile the turbulent flow. For example, by measuring the velocity at a point of a turbulent flow through a cylinder:

Then by taking an average, we obtain a Laminar flow

Because Laminar flows are well-behaved, we can model turbulent flows by their fluctuations from their Laminar profile. Before we do this in detail, it is noteworthy that the type of averaging is irrelevent, it could be a long-time average, spatial average (average over a spatial domain at an instant in time) or ensemble averages (taken over many identical experiments).

Reynold's Averaging:

We start by writing the instantaneous velocity, v, as

\(\hspace{2cm}v = \langle v\rangle + v'\)

where \(\langle v\rangle\) is the mean flow and \(v'\) is the turbulent part fluctuation. By taking the average of both sides and using linearity we see that \(\langle v'\rangle = 0\). Furthermore, we compute,

\(\hspace{2cm} \langle v^2\rangle = \langle \langle v\rangle^2 + 2\langle v\rangle v' + v'^2 \rangle = \langle v\rangle^2 + \langle v'^2\rangle\)

We state the rules of Reynold's averaging below

\(\hspace{2cm}\begin{cases} &\langle v'\rangle = 0,\\ &\langle \langle v\rangle\rangle = \langle v\rangle,\\ &\langle\langle v\rangle v'\rangle = 0,\\ &\langle v'^2\rangle = \langle v^2\rangle - \langle v\rangle^2 \neq 0,\text{ and}\\ &\langle\partial_x v\rangle = \partial_x \langle v\rangle. \end{cases}\)

We will first take a Reynold's averaging of the continuity equation - this will be useful later.
For incompressible flows, we have the continuity equation: \(\nabla \cdot v = 0\). By using the Reynold's decomposition \(v_i = \langle v_i \rangle + v'_i\) and taking an average, we compute

\(\hspace{2cm}\begin{align} &\langle \partial_i \langle v_i \rangle + \partial_i v'_i \rangle = 0\\ &\partial_i \langle v_i \rangle + 0 = 0 \end{align}\)

Hence we arive at the Reynolds averaged continuity equations:

\(\hspace{2cm} \partial_i \langle v_i\rangle \text{ and } \partial_i v'_i = 0\)

Deriving Reynold's Stress

The Reynolds stress is an artificial stress that is responsible for the turbulence of the flow. Having dimensions of velocity squared, it is not a real stress.
We start with applying the Reynolds decompositions \(v = \langle v\rangle + v'\) and \(p = \langle p\rangle + p'\) to the Euler equation.

\(\hspace{1cm}\begin{align} \partial_t v_i + v_j \partial_j v_i + \partial_i p = 0&\\ \partial_t(\langle v_i\rangle + v'_i) + (\langle v_j \rangle + v_j')\partial_j (\langle v_i \rangle + v_i') + \partial_i (\langle p \rangle + p') = 0&\\ \partial_t \langle v_i \rangle + \partial_t v_i' + \langle v_j\rangle \partial_j \langle v_i\rangle + \langle v_j\rangle \partial_j v_i' + v_j' \partial_j \langle v_i\rangle + v_j' \partial_j v_i' + \partial_i \langle p\rangle + \partial_i p'= 0& \end{align}\)

Averaging this and cancelling averages of fluctuations, we reach

\(\hspace{3cm}\partial \langle v_i\rangle + \langle v_j\rangle \partial_j \langle v_i\rangle + \langle v_j' \partial_j v_i'\rangle + \partial_i \langle p\rangle = 0\)

Finally, by use of the continuity equation,

\(\hspace{3cm}\partial_t \langle v_i \rangle + \langle v_j \rangle \partial_j \langle v_i \rangle + \partial_i \langle p \rangle = -\partial_j \langle v_j' v_i'\rangle\)

We have reached an Euler equation for the average flows, but with the additional term of \(\partial_j \langle v_j' v_i' \rangle\). This term is the derivative of the Reynolds stress tensor and is responsible for the turbulent part of the flow. Note that \(R_{ij} := \langle v_i' v_j'\rangle\) acts as a stress would but it is not a real stress, it is an artifact of averaging the nonlinearity in the Euler equation.

The Closure Problem

For the Reynolds stress tensor in matrix form

\(\hspace{2cm}R_{ij} = \langle v_i' v_j' \rangle = \begin{pmatrix} \langle v_1'^2 \rangle & \langle v_1' v_2' \rangle & \langle v_1' v_3' \rangle\\ \langle v_2' v_1' \rangle & \langle v_2'^2 \rangle & \langle v_2' v_3' \rangle\\ \langle v_3' v_1' \rangle & \langle v_3' v_2' \rangle & \langle v_3'^2 \rangle \end{pmatrix}\)

there are 6 unknowns (due to symmetry). The closure problem arises when we count that we only have 4 equations (3 momentum and 1 continuity) in our system - we do not have enough information!

Reynold's Stress Equation

We can find a transport equation for the Reynolds stress by first considering the fluctuation equation (Euler equations - Reynolds averaged Euler equations):

\(\hspace{2cm}\begin{align} \partial_t v_i + v_j \partial_j v_i + \partial_i p - \partial_t \langle v_i \rangle - \langle v_j \rangle \partial_j \langle v_i \rangle - \partial_i \langle p \rangle &= \partial_j R_{ij}\\ \partial_t v_i' + (\langle v_j\rangle + v_j') \partial_j (\langle v_i\rangle + v_i') - \langle v_j \rangle \partial_j \langle v_i \rangle + \partial_i p' &= \partial_j R_{ij}\\ \partial_t v_i' + v_j' \partial_j \langle v_i\rangle + \langle v_j\rangle \partial_j v_i' + v_j' \partial_j v_i' + \partial_i p' &= \partial_j R_{ij}\\ v_k' \partial_t v_i' + v_k' v_j' \partial_j \langle v_i\rangle + v_k' \langle v_j\rangle \partial_j v_i' + v_k' v_j' \partial_j v_i' + v_k' \partial_i p' &= v_k' \partial_j R_{ij}, &&\hspace{1cm}\text{multiplying by }v_k'.\\ \langle v_k'\partial_t v_i'\rangle + R_{kj} \partial_j \langle v_i\rangle + \langle v_j\rangle\langle v_k'\partial_j v_i'\rangle + \langle v_k' v_j' \partial_j v_i'\rangle + \langle v_k' \partial_i p'\rangle &= 0, &&\hspace{1cm}\text{averaging and cancelling averages of fluctuations.}\\ \end{align}\)

Interchanging the free indices \(i\) and \(k\), we obtain the equation

\(\hspace{2cm}\langle v_i'\partial_t v_k'\rangle + R_{ij} \partial_j \langle v_k\rangle + \langle v_j\rangle\langle i'\partial_j v_k'\rangle + \langle v_i' v_j' \partial_j v_k'\rangle + \langle v_i' \partial_k p'\rangle = 0\)

and adding this to the previous equation, we arrive at the Reynolds stress equation,

\(\hspace{2cm}\partial_t R_{ik} + \langle v_j\rangle\partial_j R_{ik} = -(\langle v_k' \partial_i p'\rangle + \langle v_i' \partial_k p'\rangle) - (\langle v_j' v_k' \partial_j v_i'\rangle + \langle v_i'v_j'\partial_j v_k'\rangle) - (R_{jk}\partial_j\langle v_i\rangle + R_{ij}\partial_j\langle v_k\rangle)\)
