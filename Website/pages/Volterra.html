The Volterra Equation

This page aims to solve the \(\mathbb{C}\)-valued Volterra integral (convolution) equation of the second kind:

\(\hspace{3cm}\rho(t) = H(t) + \int_0^t K(t-\tau)\rho(\tau)~d\tau.\)

We shall do this with the following lemma:
Volterra Lemma:
Assume that \(e^{-\mu t}H \in L^1 \cap L^2 (0,T)\) and that \(e^{-\mu t} K \in L^1 \cap L^2 (\mathbb{R}_+)\) for some \(\mu \in \mathbb{R}\). Moreover, assume that

\(\hspace{3cm} \inf_{z\in\mathbb{C}: \Re z>\mu}\left| 1-\hat{K}(z) \right| \geq \kappa > 0\)

and that for all \(\gamma > \mu, \hat{K}(\gamma + i\cdot) \in L^1\). Then, there exists a unique solution \(\rho:[0,T) \rightarrow \mathbb{C} \) with \(e^{-\mu t}\rho \in L^2(0,T)\) and for all \(z\) with \(\Re z \geq \mu\), there holds the following (extending \(H\) by zero for \( t > T \)),

\(\hspace{3cm} \hat{\rho} (z) = \frac{\hat{H}(z)}{1-\hat{K}(z)},\)

and

\(\hspace{3cm}\|e^{-\mu t}\rho\|_{L_t^2 (0,T)}\lesssim \frac{1}{\kappa}\| e^{-\mu t} H\|_{L_t^2 (0,T)}.\)

Furthermore, if we define

\(\hspace{3cm}\hat{R}(z) = \frac{\hat{K}}{1-\hat{K}},\)

then we have the solution

\(\hspace{3cm}\rho(t) = H(t) + \int_0^t R(t-\tau) H(\tau)d\tau, \)

where \(|R(t)| \lesssim e^{\mu t/2}\).

We start by taking the Laplace transform of the Volterra equation

\( \hspace{3cm} \begin{align*} \tilde{\rho}(z) &= \mathcal{L}\left[ H(t) + \int_0^t \mathcal{K} (t-\tau)\rho (\tau) ~d\tau \right]\\ &= \tilde{H}(z) + \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^\infty \int_0^t e^{-zt} \mathcal{K} (t-\tau)\rho(\tau)~d\tau dt\\ &= \tilde{H}(z) + \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^\infty \int_0^\infty \mathbb{1}_{\tau < t} e^{-z\tau} e^{-z(t-\tau)} \mathcal{K} (t-\tau)\rho(\tau)~dt d\tau\\ &= \tilde{H}(z) + \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-zs} \mathcal{K} (s)ds \int_0^\infty e^{-z\tau}\rho(\tau)~d\tau\\ &= \tilde{H}(z) + 2\pi \tilde{\mathcal{K}}(z)\tilde{\rho}(z) \end{align*} \)

To go on to solve this form of the equation, we must take an inverse laplace transform of \(\hat{\rho}\). For this we need to prove that such a \(\rho\) does indeed exist. We shall do this by a fixed point theorem argument:
Define \(F[\rho]\) by \(\tilde{F}[\rho](z) = \tilde{H}(z) + 2\pi \tilde{\mathcal{K}}(z)\tilde{\rho}(z)\) and, using the norm \(\|\cdot\|^2 = \int |\cdot|^2 e^{-2\gamma t}~dt\) for some \(\gamma\), we can show that, under this norm, \(F\) is a contraction map:

\(\begin{align*} \|F\left[\rho_1\right] - F\left[\rho_2\right]\|^2 &= \int_0^\infty |F\left[\rho_1\right](t) - F\left[\rho_2\right](t)|^2 e^{-2\gamma t}~dt\\ &= \int_{\gamma-i\infty}^{\gamma+i\infty} |\tilde{F}\left[\rho_1\right](z) - \tilde{F}\left[\rho_2\right](z)|^2 ~dz\\ &= |2\pi|^2 \int_{\gamma-i\infty}^{\gamma+i\infty} |\tilde{\mathcal{K}}(z)\tilde{\rho_1}(z) - \tilde{\mathcal{K}}(z)\tilde{\rho_2}(z)|^2 ~dz\\ &\leq |2\pi|^2 \int_{\gamma-i\infty}^{\gamma+i\infty} |\tilde{\mathcal{K}}(z)|^2 ~dz \cdot \int_{\gamma-i\infty}^{\gamma+i\infty} |\tilde{\rho_1}(z) - \tilde{\rho_2}(z)|^2 ~dz\\ &= |2\pi|^2 \int_0^\infty |\mathcal{K}(t)|^2 e^{-2\gamma t} ~dt \cdot \|\rho_1 - \rho_2 \|^2 \end{align*}\)

and so for sufficiently large \(\gamma\) (s.t. \(|2\pi|^2\int |\mathcal{K}|^2 e^{-2\gamma\cdot} < 1\)), this \(F\) is a contraction map.
By the contraction mapping theorem, there exists a unique \(\rho\) solving \(\rho = F[\rho]\) and hence the Volterra equation.
We now arrive at our solution by taking an inverse laplace transform:

\(\hspace{3cm}\begin{align*} &\tilde{\rho}(z) = \frac{\tilde{H}(z)}{1-2\pi\tilde{\mathcal{K}}(z)}\\ &\rho(t,x) = \int_{\gamma-i\infty}^{\gamma+i\infty} e^{zt}\frac{\tilde{H}(z)}{1-2\pi\tilde{\mathcal{K}}(z)}~dz \end{align*}\)
